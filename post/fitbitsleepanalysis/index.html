Fitbit Sleep Analysis

29/11/2021 3-minute read

Analysing my quality of sleep and activity levels during different parts of my life through linear regression and non-parametrics tests.

Backstory

I bought a Fitbit during my last semester (9th Feb 2021) of university with the intention of tracking and analysing my sleep quality to improve productivity. However, now that I’ve started powerlifting, I wanted to know if sleep has improved due to physical exertion.

Project Summary

  • Extracted 261 days of fitbit json files through an API to analyse activity and sleeping patterns of different parts of my life through Chi Squared and Wilcoxon Signed Rank tests
  • Built linear regression models to identify sleep induced and efficiency metrics that predicted 7 hours of daily sleep to be optimal and restless being the factor that hindered my quality of sleep

Project Results

Physical consistency

The absence of outliers in calories burnt after powerlifting proves that once I started powerlifting, I built physical structure and consistency. I train 4 days a week (Monday, Wednesday, Friday and Saturday); the calories burnt during those days are the higher than my rest days.

Q1. Has my sleep quality changed over my timeline?

Since the data was categorical, I used the Chi-Squared test for independence. My Null and Alternative hypothesis were:

H0: No relationship present between the categorical values.

H1: Relationship present.

After running the test, I attained a p-value > 0.05. Meaning that there isn’t sufficient evidence to reject my null hypothesis.

Q2. Has powerlifting made a difference in sleep?

Confirmed that the data distribution was not normal through a histogram, Shapiro-wilk test and a QQ-plot. Ran a Wilcoxon Signed Rank Test.

H0: No difference in overall sleep score once I started lifting.

H1: There is a difference.

I attained a p-value > 0.05 meaning that there isn’t enough evidence to reject my null hypothesis.

Q3. Do I build a sleep deficit?

I feature engineered sleep deficit and given that it was normally distributed, I ran a linear regression and achieved a statistically significant result. I do sleep longer the following day if I don’t get enough sleep the previous night. Now my sweet spot would be about 7 hours of sleep a day.

Q4. Is amount or quality of sleep more important?

good_sleep is a value that takes the value 1 when my overall sleep score is 80 or greater. Anything below that and I always recall waking up groggy and feeling sick-like.

I ran a logistic regression model but was not able to consider sleep_efficiency since it wasn’t statistically significant. However, having uninterrupted sleep is more important than interrupted sleep. Can confirm that 1 hour of interrupted sleep (wake_time) decreases the odds of good_sleep by 97.7%.
